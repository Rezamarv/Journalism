AP Style Guide

Essential Rules for Journalism Students

Title Case

Basic Rule

Capitalize the first word, last word, and all major words (nouns, pronouns, verbs, adverbs, adjectives) in titles. Do not capitalize articles (a, an, the), coordinating conjunctions (and, but, or, nor, for, so, yet), or prepositions of three letters or fewer (in, on, at, by, for, etc.).

Examples:

✓ "The Art of War"
✗ "The Art Of War"
✓ "Gone with the Wind"
✗ "Gone With The Wind"
✓ "One Flew Over the Cuckoo's Nest"
✗ "One Flew over the Cuckoo's nest"
Pro Tip: Always capitalize the first and last words, regardless of what they are. Prepositions of four or more letters should be capitalized (Through, Between, Among).

Abbreviations and Acronyms

General Rule

Spell out most abbreviations on first reference, followed by the abbreviation in parentheses. Use the abbreviation on subsequent references.

Examples:

✓ First reference: "The Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI) announced..."
✓ Second reference: "The FBI said..."
Common Exceptions (No Periods)

FBI, CIA, NFL, NBA, MLB, UN, US (as adjective), TV, DVD, GPS, CEO, CFO, PhD

State Abbreviations

Use postal abbreviations only in lists, tabular material, and addresses. In text, spell out state names or use AP state abbreviations.

State Examples:

✓ "Boston, Mass." (in datelines)
✓ "Boston, Massachusetts" (in text)
✗ "Boston, MA" (in text)

Books, Periodicals & Compositions

Books, Movies, TV Shows, Albums

Use quotation marks around titles of books, movies, TV shows, songs, poems, and other compositions.

Examples:

✓ "To Kill a Mockingbird"
✓ "The New York Times"
✓ "60 Minutes"
✓ "Bohemian Rhapsody"
Newspapers and Magazines

Use quotation marks around newspaper and magazine names. Do not use "The" unless it's part of the official name.

Publication Examples:

✓ "Washington Post"
✗ "The Washington Post"
✓ "The New York Times" (official name includes "The")
Remember: AP Style uses quotation marks instead of italics for most titles, unlike other style guides.

Dates, Months, Years & Times

Months

Abbreviate months with six or more letters when used with a specific date. Always spell out March, April, May, June, July.

Month Examples:

✓ Jan. 15, 2024
✓ March 15, 2024
✓ May 2024
✗ Mar. 15, 2024
Days of the Week

Abbreviate days of the week only in tabular material: Mon, Tue, Wed, Thu, Fri, Sat, Sun

Times

Use figures for times. Use a.m. and p.m. (lowercase with periods). Avoid redundant "o'clock."

Time Examples:

✓ 8 a.m.
✓ 6:30 p.m.
✓ noon
✓ midnight
✗ 8:00 a.m.
✗ 8 o'clock a.m.

Dimensions

Basic Rule

Use figures and spell out inches, feet, yards, etc. Hyphenate when used as adjectives before a noun.

Examples:

✓ The room is 9 feet by 12 feet.
✓ He is 6 feet tall.
✓ A 6-foot-tall man
✓ A 9-by-12 room
Abbreviations in Technical Copy

In technical or scientific contexts, you may use abbreviations: ft., in., yd., etc.

Key Point: Hyphenate compound adjectives but not when the dimension follows the verb.

Names

First Reference

Use full names on first reference. For adults, use courtesy titles (Mr., Ms., Mrs.) only in direct quotations or when specifically requested.

Examples:

✓ First reference: "John Smith said..."
✓ Second reference: "Smith said..."
Professional Titles

Capitalize formal titles when they appear before names. Lowercase when they appear after names or are used alone.

Title Examples:

✓ President Joe Biden
✓ Joe Biden, president of the United States
✓ The president said...
Ages

Always use figures for ages. Use hyphens for ages used as adjectives before a noun.

Age Examples:

✓ John Smith, 45, said...
✓ The 16-year-old student
✓ She is 16 years old

Numerals

Basic Rule

Spell out numbers one through nine. Use figures for 10 and above.

Examples:

✓ three cats
✓ 15 dogs
✓ nine people
✓ 10 cars
Exceptions - Always Use Figures

Ages, dates, dimensions, percentages, temperatures, times, votes, years, money, addresses, highways

Exception Examples:

✓ 5 years old
✓ 3 percent
✓ 8 a.m.
✓ $5
✓ 7 degrees
Large Numbers

Use million, billion, trillion for large round numbers. Mix figures and words for clarity.

Large Number Examples:

✓ $2.5 million
✓ 1.2 billion people
✗ $2,500,000
